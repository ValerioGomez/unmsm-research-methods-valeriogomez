This document compiles the core peer-reviewed publications selected for the literature review on botanical classification, medicinal plant prediction, growth habits, and machine learning methodologies.

## Selected Publications

### 1. MediFlora-Net: Quantum-enhanced deep learning for precision medicinal plant identification
* **Authors**: Vuma et al.
* **Year**: 2025
* **Journal**: *Biomedical Signal Processing and Control* (Elsevier)
* **DOI**: [10.1016/j.bspc.2025.107955](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bspc.2025.107955)
* **URL**: [ScienceDirect Article](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1476927125003354?via%3Dihub)
* **Summary**: Proposes a hybrid machine learning model combining Vision Transformers (ViT), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for high-precision medicinal plant identification. The study incorporates quantum-enhanced feature extraction techniques to improve classification boundaries on complex botanical images.

### 2. Machine learning enhances prediction of plants as potential sources of antimalarials
* **Authors**: Richard-Bollans A., Aitken C., [...] Howes M. J. R.
* **Year**: 2023
* **Journal**: *Frontiers in Plant Science* (Open Access)
* **DOI**: [10.3389/fpls.2023.1173328](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpls.2023.1173328)
* **URL**: [Frontiers Article](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/plant-science/articles/10.3389/fpls.2023.1173328/full)
* **Summary**: Plants are vital sources of bioactive compounds, but conventional search based only on traditional ethnobotanical knowledge is restricted. This paper presents a dataset on antiplasmodial activity for three flowering families (Apocynaceae, Loganiaceae, Rubiaceae - ~21,100 species) and compares ML models (SVM, Logistic Regression, Gradient Boosted Trees, Bayesian Neural Networks) against ethnobotanical selection. When correcting for sampling biases using reweighting, the SVM classifier performed best (precision of 0.67 vs 0.46 for the baseline). It estimates that 7,677 species warrant further investigation, showing that ML can successfully leverage ethnobotanical and plant trait data to uncover novel compounds.

### 3. Instance-level phenotype-based growth stage classification of basil in multi-plant environments
* **Authors**: Kim, Shin & Chung
* **Year**: 2025
* **Journal**: *Frontiers in Plant Science* (Vol. 16)
* **DOI**: [10.3389/fpls.2025.1707985](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpls.2025.1707985)
* **URL**: [Frontiers Article](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/plant-science/articles/10.3389/fpls.2025.1707985/full)
* **Summary**: Addresses growth stage classification of basil in multi-plant environments. The methodology utilizes a YOLO object detector for instance-level plant segmentation combined with a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) regressor to classify phenotypic growth stages, achieving a classification accuracy exceeding 98%.

### 4. On growth habits and forms: the utility of stem anatomy to define growth habits of Melastomataceae
* **Authors**: Naranjo, Torres & Quijano-Abril
* **Year**: 2018
* **Journal**: *Acta Botánica Mexicana* (No. 123, pp. 67-101)
* **DOI**: [10.21829/abm123.2018.1245](https://doi.org/10.21829/abm123.2018.1245)
* **URL**: [Acta Botánica Mexicana Article](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1439609204700045?pes=vor&utm_source=mendeley&getft_integrator=mendeley)
* **Summary**: Analyzes whether internal stem anatomical structures vary consistently across different growth habits (trees/shrubs, herbs, climbers) within the Melastomataceae family. The study concludes that anatomical wood traits can successfully group growth habits into three major morphological clusters, supporting the idea that habit is an objective, physically-linked biological classification.

### 5. Multi-Level Classifier for Medicinal Plant Type and Health Prediction
* **Authors**: Awasthi et al.
* **Year**: 2024
* **Journal**: *African Journal of Biomedical Research* (Vol. 27, Issue 65)
* **URL**: [Mendeley Catalog Link](https://www.mendeley.com/catalogue/d23d68d2-ee06-321b-9670-ebd36424b22b/)
* **Summary**: Compares several supervised machine learning classifiers (SVM, Naïve Bayes, Logistic Regression, K-Nearest Neighbors, Random Forest, and XGBoost) for classifying medicinal plant species and detecting leaf diseases. The Random Forest model achieved the highest performance, yielding an accuracy of 86.32% and an F1-score of 86.22%.

### 6. Comparison of SVM, Naive Bayes, and ELM Models in Plant Growth Classification
* **Authors**: Muflih et al.
* **Year**: 2024
* **Journal**: *INTI Journal* (No. 1)
* **URL**: [INTI Journal Article](https://iuojs.intimal.edu.my/index.php/jods/article/view/596/596)
* **Summary**: Evaluates Support Vector Machines (SVM), Naïve Bayes, and Extreme Learning Machine (ELM) models for classifying plant growth parameters. The input features include categorical variables (soil type, watering frequency) and numerical features (sunlight hours, temperature). The SVM model outperformed the others with an accuracy of 58.97%, compared to Naïve Bayes (51.28%) and ELM (43.85%).

### 7. ViewSparsifier: Killing Redundancy in Multi-View Plant Phenotyping
* **Authors**: Kampa, Deuser, Habel & Oswald
* **Year**: 2025
* **Publication**: *arXiv / GroMo 2025 Challenge*
* **DOI**: [10.1145/3746027.3762085](https://doi.org/10.1145/3746027.3762085)
* **URL**: [ACM Digital Library PDF](https://dl.acm.org/doi/epdf/10.1145/3746027.3762085)
* **Summary**: Details the "ViewSparsifier" method designed to eliminate feature redundancy in multi-view plant phenotyping. Using Vision Transformers (ViT) and learned positional encodings, the model dynamically filters redundant views, achieving first place in both phenotyping tracks of the GroMo 2025 Challenge.
